This quote from the book ‘The Immortal Life of Henrietta Lacks’, explains how Sadie uses AAVE while she’s talking about Henrietta. Everyone around Henrietta spoke the same exact way that she did when she was still alive.



Skloot, Rebecca. “The Immortal Life of Henrietta Lacks.” Broadway Books, pg. 23. This quote from the book is from Sadie again, shows how much AAVE actually is incorporated into everyday conversations in Henrietta’s time.



Skloot, Rebecca. “The Immortal Life of Henrietta Lacks.” Broadway Books, pg. 31. This quote from the book shows how Henrietta uses AAVE when she’s talking to her husband about her doctor’s visit.



Whitney, Jessica. “Five Easy Pieces: Steps toward Integrating AAVE into the Classroom.” The English Journal, 1 May 2005, www.jstor.org/stable/30047356?seq=1#page_scan_tab_contents. In this article, the author Jessica Whitney explains why incorporating AAVE into the classroom is important. According to this article, AAVE can be used as a mechanism to click better with students and get them engaged in class.



Smith, Jamil. “A Linguist Offers a Brief Immersion in Black English.” The New York Times, The New York Times, 20 Jan. 2017, www.nytimes.com/2017/01/20/books/review/talking-back-talking-black-john-mcwhorter.html. In this article, John McWhorter explains why he thinks African American Vernacular English is a very valid dialect of Standard English. He talks about how the other side believes AAVE is a “bunch of slang” and “illiterate sentences” thrown together, but he elaborates on why its not that.
