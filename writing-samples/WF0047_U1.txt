Media​ ​Literacy
Media​ ​today​ ​is​ ​not​ ​always​ ​reliable.​ ​Consumers​ ​of​ ​different​ ​medias​​ ​should​ ​know​ ​not​ ​to
trust​ ​everything​ ​they​ ​read​ ​and​ ​should​ ​be​ ​aware​ ​of​ ​things​ ​like​ ​“fake​ ​news”.​ ​The​ ​Federal​ ​Trade Commision​ ​requires​ ​advertisements​ ​to​ ​be​ ​truthful​ ​and​ ​not​ ​lead​ ​viewers​ ​astray,​ ​but​ ​this​ ​is​ ​not always​ ​strictly​ ​enforced.​ ​In​ ​order​ ​for​ ​people​ ​to​ ​be​ ​active​ ​citizens​ ​in​ ​their​ ​societies,​ ​they​ ​must​ ​be engaged​ ​with​ ​what​ ​they​ ​are​ ​viewing​ ​and​ ​be​ ​able​ ​to​ ​critically​ ​analyze.​ ​Modern​ ​media​ ​does​ ​not​ ​do things​ ​to​ ​people,​ ​but​ ​people​ ​do​ ​things​ ​with​ ​media.​ ​Advertising​ ​​ ​exists​ ​to​ ​try​ ​and​ ​convince viewers​ ​to​ ​take​ ​action​ ​in​ ​something​ ​whether​ ​it​ ​is​ ​buying​ ​a​ ​product​ ​or​ ​forming​ ​a​ ​specific​ ​opinion or​ ​belief.​ ​News​ ​media​ ​exists​ ​to​ ​inform​ ​and​ ​sometimes​ ​explain.​ ​Social​ ​media​ ​exists​ ​to​ ​help​ ​us​ ​all connect​ ​with​ ​each​ ​other.​ ​Many​ ​times​ ​the​ ​borders​ ​of​ ​these​ ​three​ ​types​ ​of​ ​media​ ​cross​ ​each​ ​other and​ ​become​ ​blurred.​ ​​ ​Consumers​ ​of​ ​media​ ​must​ ​be​ ​aware​ ​of​ ​the​ ​detail​ ​that​ ​goes​ ​into​ ​all​ ​media and​ ​the​ ​ways​ ​it​ ​is​ ​designed​ ​to​ ​persuade,​ ​or​ ​inform,​ ​or​ ​offer​ ​connection.​ ​It​ ​should​ ​inherently​ ​be the​ ​consumers​ ​job​ ​to​ ​interpret​ ​and​ ​analyze​ ​what​ ​they​ ​are​ ​viewing​ ​as​ ​well​ ​as​ ​be​ ​aware​ ​of​ ​the extreme​ ​effect​ ​that​ ​the​ ​media​ ​has​ ​on​ ​society.
Media​ ​is​ ​a​ ​part​ ​of​ ​everyone's​ ​life​ ​now,​ ​even​ ​when​ ​we​ ​do​ ​not​ ​realize​ ​it.​ ​Media​ ​can​ ​be television,​ ​social​ ​media,​ ​billboards,​ ​magazines,​ ​or​ ​newspapers.​ ​No​ ​matter​ ​where​ ​we​ ​go,​ ​we​ ​are constantly​ ​being​ ​thrown​ ​some​ ​sort​ ​of​ ​advertisement,​ ​opinion,​ ​or​ ​information.​ ​In​ ​order​ ​to​ ​become an​ ​informed​ ​citizen​ ​we​ ​have​ ​to​ ​know​ ​how​ ​to​ ​decipher​ ​these​ ​messages.​ ​In​ ​the​ ​article​ ​“Media Literacy​ ​as​ ​a​ ​Core​ ​Competency​ ​for​ ​Engaged​ ​Citizenship​ ​in​ ​Participatory​ ​Democracy”​ ​by​ ​Paul Mihailidis​ ​and​ ​Benjamin​ ​Thevenin​ ​they​ ​quote​ ​that​ ​​ ​“Widespread​ ​media​ ​literacy​ ​is​ ​essential​ ​if​ ​all
￼citizens​ ​are​ ​to​ ​wield​ ​power,​ ​make​ ​rational​ ​decisions,​ ​become​ ​effective​ ​change​ ​agents,​ ​and​ ​have an​ ​effective​ ​involvement​ ​with​ ​the​ ​media​ ​(1614).​ ​As​ ​we​ ​grow​ ​to​ ​rely​ ​more​ ​and​ ​more​ ​on​ ​media​ ​for our​ ​information​ ​in​ ​regards​ ​to​ ​news​ ​or​ ​research,​ ​there​ ​must​ ​be​ ​some​ ​sort​ ​of​ ​critical​ ​analysis​ ​to break​ ​down​ ​the​ ​constant​ ​flow​ ​of​ ​information​ ​into​ ​true​ ​or​ ​false,​ ​or​ ​in​ ​some​ ​cases​ ​something​ ​in between.​ ​The​ ​concentration​ ​of​ ​media​ ​ownership​ ​in​ ​our​ ​society​ ​consists​ ​widely​ ​of​ ​the​ ​“big​ ​six”. These​ ​six​ ​mega​ ​companies​ ​put​ ​out​ ​the​ ​majority​ ​of​ ​content​ ​that​ ​we​ ​see​ ​as​ ​consumers,​ ​which means​ ​that​ ​there​ ​are​ ​largely​ ​only​ ​six​ ​different​ ​opinions​ ​or​ ​beliefs​ ​being​ ​produced.​ ​Consumers must​ ​be​ ​able​ ​to​ ​critically​ ​analyze​ ​every​ ​aspect​ ​that​ ​goes​ ​into​ ​the​ ​media​ ​they​ ​encounter​ ​in​ ​order to​ ​be​ ​as​ ​correctly​ ​informed​ ​as​ ​they​ ​possibly​ ​can.​ ​Everything​ ​should​ ​be​ ​critiqued​ ​and​ ​analyzed​ ​in a​ ​community​ ​where​ ​media​ ​consumption​ ​is​ ​so​ ​high.
In​ ​order​ ​to​ ​critically​ ​analyze​ ​the​ ​media​ ​in​ ​our​ ​lives,​ ​consumers​ ​have​ ​to​ ​be​ ​aware​ ​of​ ​what goes​ ​into​ ​creating​ ​the​ ​media.​ ​In​ ​addition​ ​to​ ​the​ ​top​ ​six​ ​companies​ ​controlling​ ​most​ ​of​ ​the​ ​modern media​ ​world,​ ​things​ ​like​ ​lighting,​ ​editing,​ ​internal​ ​language,​ ​special​ ​effects,​ ​and​ ​editing​ ​all contribute​ ​to​ ​the​ ​media's​ ​overall​ ​message.​ ​In​ ​the​ ​article​ ​by​ ​Sue​ ​Shellenbarger​ ​“Most​ ​Students Don't​ ​Know​ ​When​ ​News​ ​Is​ ​Fake,​ ​Stanford​ ​Study​ ​Finds;​ ​Teens​ ​absorb​ ​social​ ​media​ ​news without​ ​considering​ ​the​ ​source;​ ​parents​ ​can​ ​teach​ ​research​ ​skills​ ​and​ ​skepticism”​ ​the​ ​largest study​ ​of​ ​its​ ​kind​ ​done​ ​by​ ​Stanford​ ​reports​ ​that​ ​out​ ​of​ ​7,804​ ​middle​ ​school​ ​to​ ​college​ ​level students,​ ​80%​ ​are​ ​not​ ​able​ ​to​ ​distinguish​ ​sponsored​ ​from​ ​unsponsored​ ​content.​ ​These​ ​students instead​ ​decide​ ​an​ ​article's​ ​credibility​ ​on​ ​how​ ​many​ ​different​ ​details​ ​are​ ​added​ ​in​ ​like​ ​large pictures​ ​(Shellenbarger).​ ​The​ ​first​ ​thing​ ​we​ ​see,​ ​and​ ​often​ ​only​ ​have​ ​time​ ​to​ ​see,​ ​is​ ​usually​ ​a picture​ ​or​ ​special​ ​effect.​ ​Naturally​ ​our​ ​eye​ ​will​ ​go​ ​to​ ​the​ ​things​ ​that​ ​pop​ ​out​ ​the​ ​most​ ​and​ ​intrigue us,​ ​but​ ​this​ ​does​ ​not​ ​mean​ ​it's​ ​true.​ ​When​ ​so​ ​many​ ​forms​ ​of​ ​media​ ​are​ ​controlled​ ​by​ ​the​ ​same people,​ ​the​ ​same​ ​thing​ ​can​ ​be​ ​seen​ ​again​ ​and​ ​again,​ ​but​ ​said​ ​in​ ​different​ ​ways.​ ​This​ ​can​ ​then convince​ ​those​ ​who​ ​continue​ ​to​ ​view​ ​these​ ​things,​ ​that​ ​it​ ​must​ ​be​ ​right.​ ​It​ ​should​ ​be​ ​important​ ​to
￼get​ ​information​ ​from​ ​many​ ​different​ ​sources​ ​and​ ​be​ ​able​ ​to​ ​check​ ​the​ ​sources​ ​too.​ ​The​ ​same article​ ​also​ ​states​ ​“More​ ​than​ ​two​ ​out​ ​of​ ​three​ ​middle-schoolers​ ​couldn't​ ​see​ ​any​ ​valid​ ​reason​ ​to mistrust​ ​a​ ​post​ ​written​ ​by​ ​a​ ​bank​ ​executive​ ​arguing​ ​that​ ​young​ ​adults​ ​need​ ​more financial-planning​ ​help”(Shellenbarger).​ ​The​ ​goal​ ​in​ ​advertising​ ​or​ ​media​ ​is​ ​to​ ​sell​ ​an​ ​idea, product,​ ​or​ ​message​ ​to​ ​the​ ​viewers.​ ​Creators​ ​of​ ​some​ ​types​ ​of​ ​media​ ​are​ ​going​ ​to​ ​use​ ​any means​ ​possible​ ​to​ ​sell​ ​themselves​ ​to​ ​people​ ​and​ ​look​ ​the​ ​best​ ​they​ ​possibly​ ​can.​ ​Due​ ​to​ ​this, consumers​ ​should​ ​do​ ​extra​ ​research​ ​into​ ​what​ ​they​ ​are​ ​looking​ ​into​ ​and​ ​make​ ​sure​ ​their sources​ ​are​ ​reliable​ ​and​ ​trustworthy​ ​along​ ​with​ ​not​ ​being​ ​biased.
Media​ ​advertisers​ ​often​ ​cross​ ​the​ ​line​ ​in​ ​regards​ ​to​ ​moral​ ​and​ ​ethical​ ​codes.​ ​In​ ​order​ ​to grab​ ​a​ ​viewer's​ ​attention,​ ​they​ ​will​ ​use​ ​any​ ​emotional​ ​or​ ​triggering​ ​message​ ​that​ ​they​ ​can. These​ ​viewers​ ​often​ ​fall​ ​into​ ​the​ ​dissonance​ ​theory​ ​that​ ​suggests​ ​that​ ​when​ ​people​ ​are​ ​faced with​ ​some​ ​sort​ ​of​ ​conflicting,​ ​uncomfortable​ ​information,​ ​they​ ​will​ ​try​ ​to​ ​reduce​ ​it.​ ​“Fake​ ​news”​ ​is not​ ​going​ ​to​ ​be​ ​going​ ​anywhere​ ​according​ ​to​ ​the​ ​article​ ​“Fake​ ​news:​ ​belief​ ​in​ ​post-truth”​ ​by​ ​Nick Rochlin.​ ​Due​ ​to​ ​the​ ​wide​ ​range​ ​of​ ​fake​ ​news​ ​sites​ ​and​ ​their​ ​ability​ ​to​ ​make​ ​money,​ ​it​ ​will​ ​be​ ​hard to​ ​eliminate​ ​them​ ​completely.​ ​Some​ ​sites​ ​make​ ​upwards​ ​of​ ​10,000​ ​dollars​ ​a​ ​post​ ​on​ ​fake​ ​news and​ ​have​ ​six​ ​to​ ​ten​ ​other​ ​websites​ ​in​ ​use​ ​as​ ​well,​ ​if​ ​their​ ​others​ ​fail​ ​(Rochlin).​ ​With​ ​so​ ​many​ ​false news​ ​and​ ​advertisements​ ​on​ ​the​ ​rise,​ ​it​ ​is​ ​almost​ ​impossible​ ​to​ ​completely​ ​eliminate​ ​the possibility​ ​of​ ​ever​ ​encountering​ ​them.​ ​Therefore,​ ​the​ ​consumer​ ​has​ ​to​ ​be​ ​media​ ​literate​ ​and critically​ ​analyze​ ​all​ ​aspects​ ​of​ ​the​ ​news.​ ​The​ ​advertising​ ​and​ ​news​ ​business​ ​is​ ​all​ ​about​ ​making money​ ​which​ ​is​ ​clearly​ ​shown​ ​through​ ​the​ ​hundreds​ ​of​ ​fake​ ​news​ ​websites​ ​that​ ​arise.​ ​Producers and​ ​editors​ ​will​ ​do​ ​anything​ ​to​ ​get​ ​viewers​ ​and​ ​readers​ ​to​ ​trust​ ​and​ ​believe​ ​them,​ ​which​ ​leads​ ​to buying​ ​the​ ​product​ ​being​ ​advertised​ ​or​ ​just​ ​simply​ ​forming​ ​an​ ​opinion​ ​or​ ​belief.​ ​Fake​ ​news​ ​falls into​ ​the​ ​emotional​ ​side​ ​of​ ​media​ ​literacy​ ​and​ ​adds​ ​more​ ​information​ ​to​ ​what​ ​people​ ​want​ ​to hear,​ ​instead​ ​of​ ​getting​ ​to​ ​the​ ​truth​ ​after​ ​deep,​ ​complex​ ​research​ ​and​ ​analyzation.
￼Society​ ​today​ ​has​ ​become​ ​ever​ ​more​ ​dependant​ ​on​ ​the​ ​latest​ ​headline,​ ​or​ ​post,​ ​or segment​ ​as​ ​a​ ​source​ ​for​ ​information.​ ​It​ ​seems​ ​that​ ​the​ ​smallest,​ ​most​ ​concise​ ​message​ ​is​ ​now the​ ​one​ ​most​ ​easily​ ​absorbed.​ ​Many​ ​of​ ​us​ ​are​ ​forming​ ​opinions​ ​about​ ​life​ ​based​ ​on​ ​280 characters​ ​or​ ​less.​ ​In​ ​our​ ​rapidly​ ​changing​ ​world,​ ​we​ ​would​ ​all​ ​be​ ​well​ ​served​ ​to​ ​investigate, analyze,​ ​and​ ​have​ ​some​ ​healthy​ ​skepticism​ ​for​ ​much​ ​of​ ​the​ ​things​ ​being​ ​pushed​ ​at​ ​us​ ​through the​ ​media.
